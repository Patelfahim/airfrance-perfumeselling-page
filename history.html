History of Air France

1933

Foundation

In the early days of commercial aviation, flying was still an adventure reserved for explorers, diplomats, and pioneers. Air France emerged in 1933 as a bold national project, uniting multiple French airlines to create a single carrier capable of representing France across continents.

The airline quickly became known for:

  • Long-distance routes over Africa and Asia
  • Skilled pilots navigating with limited technology
  • Aircraft that combined engineering with French craftsmanship
  • Pioneering night flights and trans-Mediterranean routes
  • Training programs for aviation crews across Europe

Air France flights during this era were not just transportation, but symbols of courage and progress. The airline employed over 2,000 staff by 1939 and operated routes to 50+ destinations worldwide.

1945

Post-War Expansion

After World War II, Air France played a vital role in reconnecting a fractured world. Nationalized in 1945, the airline expanded rapidly, introducing pressurized cabins, improved safety standards, and new aircraft designed for comfort and reliability.

Key achievements included:

  • Introduction of Douglas DC-4 aircraft for transatlantic routes
  • Establishment of Paris-New York daily service by 1950
  • Development of luxury cabin classes and meal services
  • Expansion to 80+ destinations across Europe, Africa, and Asia
  • Training center established in Paris for pilot and crew development

Paris became a key aviation hub, and Air France earned a reputation for professionalism and refined onboard service that lasted decades.

1976

The Concorde Era

Air France began operating the supersonic Concorde, reducing travel time between Paris and New York to under 3.5 hours. This iconic aircraft represented the pinnacle of aviation innovation.

  • Only 20 Concorde aircraft were ever built (7 for Air France)
  • Cruised at Mach 2 (twice the speed of sound)
  • Exclusively served elite passengers on premium routes
  • Became a symbol of French engineering excellence
  • Operated until 2003, after which it was retired

The Concorde era elevated Air France to legendary status in aviation history.

2004

Air France–KLM Merger

Air France merged with KLM Royal Dutch Airlines to form Air France–KLM, one of the world's largest airline groups by revenue and passenger traffic.

Strategic benefits included:

  • Combined fleet of over 900 aircraft
  • Enhanced connectivity across Europe and beyond
  • Shared resources and operational efficiency
  • Expanded cargo and logistics operations
  • Dual hub strategy: Paris (CDG) and Amsterdam (AMS)

This partnership strengthened Air France's global presence and competitive positioning in the aviation industry.

Today

Modern & Sustainable

Air France operates modern aircraft like the Airbus A350 and invests heavily in sustainability, digital services, and premium passenger experience.

Current initiatives include:

  • Fleet modernization with fuel-efficient aircraft
  • Carbon-neutral travel options and sustainability goals
  • Digital transformation for seamless booking and boarding
  • Premium lounges and enhanced cabin comfort
  • Expansion of regional routes with partners
  • Investment in sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) research

With over 90 years of history, Air France continues to set industry standards for safety, service quality, and innovation.
